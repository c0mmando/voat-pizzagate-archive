The most common reason for sealing an indictment is the need to avoid tipping off the defendant (in this case, all 12 of them) who might otherwise become fugitives. Another reason is to keep an indictment out of the media spotlight for as long as possible. The SOL (statute of limitations) is tolled, so the filing under seal is not needed to obtain a SOL benefit. What about expungement for felony convictions after a waiting period? Expungement is the process of getting your criminal records sealed, destroyed, or cleared.

At the federal level, there is no overreaching federal staute governing whether courts may grant expungement orders. A patchwork of statutes governs the rules for the expungement of records in "special circumstances." Expungement is not based on the type of crime committed, but in which circuit the crime is committed. For example, at the federal level = FBI, which raises the question of whether courts have the power to expunge executive branch records.

While there is no uniform process known as expungement, it is commonly accepted that the purpose of it is to conceal criminal records from the public by destroying, erasing, or by sealing arrest or conviction records.

In the District of Columbia, one can petition to have their criminal records sealed if you can show that you were innocent of the crime for which you were arrested OR charged.
